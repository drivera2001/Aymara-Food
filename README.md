Traditional food systems of indigenous communities contain sophisticated ecological knowledge for managing food security in marginal environments, yet their documentation remains incomplete. This ethnobotanical study characterizes food plant diversity and traditional processing methods used by Aymara communities in Bolivia's altiplano, emphasizing detoxification protocols for naturally toxic plants. Fieldwork conducted between 2001 and 2015 involved semi-structured interviews with 287 informants following Prior Informed Consent procedures, complemented by participatory observation. The study documented 195 plant species across 71 families, generating over 8,000 utilization records. Solanaceae dominated with approximately 2,000 records representing 24 species, reflecting potato diversity's centrality in highland agriculture. Autochthonous plants constituted 70% of records (approximately 5,800 records, 115 species), demonstrating Andean food systems' foundation on locally evolved biodiversity. Analysis identified eight processing categories including sophisticated detoxification protocols: alkaline ash-cooking for glycoalkaloid neutralization, freeze-thaw cycles for chuño production, extended fermentation-washing for tunta achieving maximum detoxification, thermal roasting, and boiling/steaming methods scaled to variety-specific toxicity levels. Tuber processing dominated with approximately 2,850 records across 70 taxa, reflecting intensive documentation of storage preparation methods essential for year-round food security. This systematic documentation provides evidence-based foundation for interventions promoting food sovereignty, nutritional security, and agrobiodiversity conservation amid accelerating environmental and cultural change.
